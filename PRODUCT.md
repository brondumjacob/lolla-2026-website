# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

Two overlapping phases of the same visitor, roughly equally weighted: fans still deciding who to see, browsing the lineup by genre and day and previewing artists they don't know; and fans who've already committed, deep in logistics — building a day-by-day schedule, checking for set-time conflicts, printing or saving a route for the day. The same person moves through both phases over the weeks before the festival, so the discovery layer (lineup, guides) and the planning layer (schedule builder, My Lineup) need to feel like one connected flow, not two separate products bolted together.

## Product Purpose

A free, no-login-required way to explore the complete Lollapalooza 2026 lineup — 172 artists, searchable by genre and day, with direct links into Spotify, Apple Music, and YouTube Music — and turn that exploration into an actual day-by-day schedule with conflict detection. Since the site is ad-supported (AdSense live site-wide), success is measured primarily by traffic and engagement depth: people returning multiple times through the festival window to refine their plan, not just a single pageview.

## Positioning

The most accurate, best-curated way to explore the Lolla 2026 lineup and turn it into a real schedule — direct streaming links, cited genre data, no app required.

## Brand Personality

Warm, playful, festival-energy — golden-hour-at-a-festival warmth, inviting and a little fun without tipping into corporate polish or clinical utility-app coldness. This is the existing "Golden Hour" visual direction (pastel pink/lilac + lime + teal accents, Righteous display type + Manrope body, soft rounded cards): the tone should read as genuinely enthusiastic about the festival, not neutral or transactional, while staying legible and useful as a real planning tool.

## Anti-references

Not a generic ticketing or corporate site (Ticketmaster/AXS-style sterility, stock-photo festival marketing). Not a cluttered fan-wiki or forum — despite covering 172 artists and four days of content, it should stay clean and legible, never dense or ad-choked. Not the generic AI-generated SaaS look — no cream/beige near-white backgrounds by default, no gradient text, no card-grid sameness, no tiny uppercase tracked eyebrows scaffolding every section.

## Design Principles

Go deep before going wide. The competitive field (Festival Dust, Festiverse) is breadth-first — many festivals, thin coverage each, app-only. This product wins by being the best possible experience for one festival first, with real data quality and editorial care, before expanding festival by festival.

Discovery and logistics are one flow, not two features. A visitor moving from "who is this artist" to "when are they playing" to "does that conflict with someone else I want to see" should never feel like they left one product and entered another.

Cited, not guessed. Genre tags, set times, and artist data carry real sources rather than best-effort guesses — accuracy is a trust signal and a differentiator against competitors who show no evidence of data rigor.

No gatekeeping on the core experience. Browsing the lineup and building a schedule works immediately in the browser, with no app install and no login wall — accounts (when added) enhance the experience by saving it, they don't gate it.

Festival warmth, not corporate polish or wiki clutter. Every surface should feel like it belongs to the festival's own energy, not to a logistics tool that happens to be festival-themed.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA baseline: sufficient color contrast throughout (particularly against the warm pastel background palette), full keyboard navigation, alt text on meaningful imagery, and no motion-only conveyance of information.
