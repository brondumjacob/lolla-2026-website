Who To See at Lolla 2026

A day-by-day guide to the best of the lineup · March 2026

Lollapalooza 2026 drops 172 artists across four days at Grant Park. That's a lot of music to sort through. We broke the lineup down day by day, picked our favorites across genres, and flagged the schedule conflicts you'll actually lose sleep over. Whether you're chasing headliners or hunting for your next obsession in the undercard, here's where to start.

Thursday, July 30 — Opening Day

Thursday's lineup is stacked with range. Lorde headlines with an Alt Pop catalog that spans a decade of critical acclaim, from the brooding minimalism of Pure Heroine to the sun-drenched textures of Solar Power. She is one of the rare headliners who commands a stage with just her voice and movement — expect an emotional, visually ambitious set.

John Summit closes out the electronic side of Thursday with a headlining slot that reflects his meteoric rise from Chicago's own house scene. There's poetic justice in a hometown DJ headlining Lolla, and his sets are known for relentless energy. If you've been anywhere near a festival in the last three years, you know what to expect: deep house grooves that build into peak-time euphoria.

In the undercard, Little Simz is the can't-miss act of the day. The London rapper has earned a Mercury Prize and widespread critical praise for albums that blend introspective lyricism with expansive, genre-fluid production. She's technically listed as undercard, which means you're getting a world-class performer on a smaller stage — take advantage of that.

Wet Leg brings their dry-witted indie rock to a major slot. Their self-titled debut was one of the most talked-about guitar records in years, and tracks like "Chaise Longue" translate into rowdy, joyful live energy. Meanwhile, Empire of the Sun offers theatrical electro-pop that feels tailor-made for a sunset slot.

Thursday Pick

Paris Paloma (Folk, Undercard) — Her viral track "labour" became an anthem in 2024 and she's built a devoted following since. Early-day folk on a small stage is a perfect way to ease into the weekend.

Schedule Conflict

Lorde and John Summit will almost certainly overlap on Thursday night. If you want both, plan to catch the first half of one and sprint to the other. Lorde tends to run tight, emotional sets while Summit builds slowly — showing up 20 minutes into Summit's set won't hurt.

Friday, July 31 — The Heavyweights

Charli XCX headlines Friday fresh off the cultural phenomenon of brat. She has spent a decade pushing pop music forward, and her live shows have evolved from chaotic club sets into full-scale production spectacles. Friday night at Grant Park will be one of the defining festival moments of 2026.

The Smashing Pumpkins offer a generational counterpoint on Friday — Billy Corgan's band brings a catalog that spans from Siamese Dream through Mellon Collie and beyond. For anyone who grew up on 90s alternative rock, this is a pilgrimage. For everyone else, the Pumpkins' wall-of-sound guitar approach still hits harder than most modern rock acts.

The major tier on Friday is deep. Freddie Gibbs brings street-level hip-hop precision refined over years of working with producers like Madlib and The Alchemist. Suki Waterhouse has crossed over from indie darling to genuine pop presence with dreamy, lo-fi guitar tracks. And I-DLE represents K-Pop's continued festival takeover — their choreography and production values are arena-level.

Friday Pick

Horsegirl (Rock, Undercard) — This Chicago trio plays angular, noisy post-punk that feels right at home in Grant Park. Catching a hometown band on a small stage is one of the best things about Lolla.

Saturday, August 1 — Peak Festival

Saturday is arguably the strongest single day of the lineup. Jennie headlines representing BLACKPINK's solo era, bringing K-Pop's biggest production values to the main stage. Olivia Dean also headlines — her Soul/Pop sound is warm, vocally stunning, and positions her as one of the most exciting new headliners in the festival world.

The rest of Saturday reads like its own festival. Ethel Cain has built one of the most devoted fanbases in alternative music with her gothic Americana and cinematic storytelling. Clipse reuniting on the major stage means Pusha T and No Malice delivering some of the sharpest rap verses of the last two decades. The Neighbourhood brings their moody, dark-pop anthems.

Geese is the Saturday act most likely to convert new fans. The Brooklyn band plays post-punk with an art-rock ambition that rewards repeated listens. Their live energy is raw and kinetic.

Schedule Conflict

Saturday's biggest conflict is likely Jennie vs. Olivia Dean in the headlining slots. Both are worth seeing in full. If forced to choose: Jennie for spectacle and choreography, Olivia Dean for pure vocal power and a more intimate connection with the crowd.

Sunday, August 2 — The Closer

Tate McRae closes out Sunday and the entire festival. She's gone from viral dance videos to genuine pop stardom, and her catalog is deep enough now to fill a headlining set with hits. The XX co-headline Sunday with their signature minimalist indie sound — the space between their notes is as important as the notes themselves. An XX set at dusk in Grant Park could be transcendent.

Sunday's major tier is packed with variety. Turnstile will deliver the most physically intense set of the weekend — their hardcore-meets-everything approach has broken the genre wide open, and their pit will be legendary. Beabadoobee brings indie-pop with a 90s guitar crunch. Hot Mulligan represents the emo revival with hooks that stick for days. And Yoasobi brings J-Pop's biggest crossover act to an American festival stage.

Don't overlook Muna on Sunday — their queer indie-pop anthems are made for festival singalongs, and they consistently deliver one of the most joyful live shows in indie music.

Sunday Pick

Water From Your Eyes (Indie, Undercard) — Experimental, unpredictable, and utterly original. If you want to see something that doesn't sound like anything else on the lineup, this is your act.

Genre Guides

Best for Pop Fans

The pop lineup runs deep beyond headliners. Charli XCX and Tate McRae anchor the top, but Zara Larsson (Fri) and Jade (Sun) bring strong pop catalogs at the major tier. In the undercard, Claire Rosinkranz and Slayyter on Friday are both worth catching early.

Best for Rock and Indie

This might be the strongest rock lineup at Lolla in years. The Smashing Pumpkins headline, Turnstile and Viagra Boys anchor the majors, and the undercard is stacked: Wolf Alice (Sat), Momma (Sat), Bad Nerves (Thu), and The Story So Far (Fri) all deliver live.

Best for Electronic and EDM

John Summit headlines Thursday, but the electronic depth goes far beyond that. Boris Brejcha (Thu) brings his signature high-tech minimal. Alison Wonderland (Sat) and Major Lazer (Fri) hold down the majors. For deeper cuts, check Ninajirachi (Thu) and Oklou (Fri) in the undercard.

Best for Hip-Hop and R&B

Freddie Gibbs and Clipse deliver lyrical precision. Lil Uzi Vert (Fri) and Mustard (Fri) bring energy. Leon Thomas (Sat) is a rising R&B star. In the undercard, Little Simz (Thu) is the consensus don't-miss pick, and Quadeca (Sat) has evolved from YouTube rapper into a genuinely boundary-pushing artist.

The Bottom Line

This is a four-day lineup with genuine depth at every tier. The headliners are strong, but Lolla 2026's real value is in the middle and lower tiers — acts like Little Simz, Geese, Wolf Alice, and Ethel Cain who are at a career inflection point where their live shows are electric and their stages are still small enough to feel personal. Build your schedule around discovery, not just the names you know, and you'll walk away with stories.
