Lolla 2026 First Timer's Guide

Practical advice for your first Lollapalooza · March 2026

Lollapalooza 2026 takes over Grant Park in downtown Chicago from Thursday, July 30 through Sunday, August 2. Four days, 172 artists, eight stages, and roughly 400,000 people across the weekend. If this is your first time, the scale can feel overwhelming. It doesn't have to be. Here's everything we wish someone had told us before our first Lolla.

The Basics

Lollapalooza is held in Grant Park, a 319-acre public park on Chicago's lakefront. The festival grounds span roughly from Congress Parkway to Balbo Drive, between Michigan Avenue and Columbus Drive. The park sits in the heart of downtown — you'll be surrounded by the Chicago skyline on all sides, which gives Lolla a visual energy that most rural festivals can't touch.

Gates typically open at 11:00 AM, and music runs until 10:00 PM. After-shows at venues across the city run until the early morning hours. A single-day general admission pass, a 4-day GA pass, GA+, and VIP tiers are all available. GA gets you in the door and access to every stage. GA+ adds dedicated viewing areas and air-conditioned restrooms. VIP adds premium food, drinks, and exclusive lounges.

Getting There

Grant Park is one of the most transit-accessible festival sites in America. The CTA (Chicago's public transit system) is the best way to get there. The Red, Blue, Brown, Green, Orange, and Pink Lines all have stops within walking distance. The closest L stops are Harrison (Red Line), Jackson (Red/Blue Lines), and Adams/Wabash (Brown/Green/Orange/Pink Lines).

Rideshare works but expect surge pricing and heavy traffic, especially at the end of the night. If you're using Uber or Lyft, set your pickup point a few blocks away from the park — the designated pickup zones get congested. Walking is underrated. If you're staying anywhere in the Loop, South Loop, or near Michigan Avenue, you can walk to the gates in under 20 minutes.

Driving is the worst option. Street parking near Grant Park is extremely limited during the festival, and garage rates in the Loop will be at their highest. If you must drive, park further out and take the L in.

Pro Tip

Buy a Ventra card (CTA fare card) before the festival and load it up. It's $2.50 per ride and transfers are $0.25. Far cheaper and faster than rideshare, and you'll avoid the post-headliner surge pricing entirely.

What to Bring

Lolla's bag policy allows one small bag (no larger than 14" x 11" x 5") or one clear bag (no larger than 19" x 13" x 7.5"). You'll go through security at the gates, and smaller bags mean faster entry. Here's what actually matters to have on you:

Water bottle — Empty, reusable bottles are allowed and there are free refill stations throughout the park. Chicago in late July averages highs around 84°F with humidity. Dehydration is the number one reason people leave early or end up at the medical tent. Drink water constantly, even if you don't feel thirsty.

Sunscreen — Grant Park has some tree cover, but most stage areas are fully exposed. Apply before you arrive and reapply. Sealed, unopened sunscreen containers are allowed through security.

Comfortable shoes — You'll walk 8-12 miles per day across grass, dirt, pavement, and (if it rains) mud. Wear shoes you don't mind getting destroyed. Broken-in sneakers or trail shoes work best. Leave the sandals at home.

Portable charger — Your phone will die. You'll be using it for the schedule app, maps, finding friends, and photos. A portable battery pack is non-negotiable. Charge it fully the night before.

Cash — Most vendors take cards, but a small amount of cash ($20-40) is useful for tipping, splitting costs, or handling card reader failures. ATMs on-site have long lines and fees.

Pro Tip

Bring a lightweight rain poncho. Chicago weather shifts fast in the summer. A poncho packs smaller than an umbrella (which may not be allowed), dries quickly, and can double as a ground cover.

Building Your Schedule

With 172 artists across four days, you cannot see everything. Accept this early and you'll have a much better time. Here's how to approach it:

Start with the headliners. Figure out which headliner you want to see each night and work backwards. Headliners are the fixed points of your schedule — everything else is flexible. This year's headliners are Lorde and John Summit (Thursday), Charli XCX and The Smashing Pumpkins (Friday), Jennie and Olivia Dean (Saturday), and Tate McRae and The XX (Sunday).

Identify 2-3 must-sees per day. Beyond your headliner pick, choose 2-3 acts you absolutely will not miss. Build buffers around these. Trying to catch every act back-to-back with zero breaks leads to burnout by Saturday.

Leave room for discovery. Some of the best festival moments come from wandering into a set you know nothing about. If you have a gap in your schedule, walk to the nearest stage and give whoever's playing 10 minutes. You might find your new favorite artist.

Account for travel time. Grant Park is large. Moving between the furthest stages can take 15-20 minutes in a crowd. Don't schedule back-to-back acts on opposite ends of the park without building in a buffer.

Reality Check

You will miss something great. Every single person at the festival will miss something great. That's the nature of a multi-stage festival. Let go of FOMO and commit to the moments you're in.

Food and Drink

Lolla's food vendor lineup is one of the best of any American festival. You'll find a range of Chicago staples and national vendors. Expect to pay festival prices — $12-18 for most entrees, $10-14 for cocktails and craft beer. The food quality is generally high. Lines are shortest in the first two hours after gates open and during mid-afternoon sets. Prime meal times (12-1 PM, 5-6 PM) mean long waits at the most popular vendors.

If you have dietary restrictions, Lolla has improved significantly on vegetarian, vegan, and gluten-free options. Check the festival app when it launches for vendor details and dietary filters.

Staying Safe

Lolla has medical tents staffed by professionals at multiple locations throughout the park. If you feel sick, dizzy, overheated, or anything else — go. There's no set worth your health. Know where the nearest medical tent is to wherever you're watching a set.

Hydrate aggressively. It bears repeating. Chicago in late July is hot and humid, and you're standing in direct sun for hours. Drink water before you feel thirsty. Alternate alcoholic drinks with water.

Wear earplugs. Seriously. Sustained exposure to concert-level sound (100+ decibels) causes permanent hearing damage. A good pair of musician's earplugs ($15-30) reduce volume without killing sound quality. You'll actually hear the music better with them in because the distortion drops away. This is the single most underrated piece of festival advice.

Have a meeting point. Cell service degrades when 100,000 people are packed into one park. Designate a physical meeting point with your group — a specific landmark, food vendor, or stage — in case texts don't go through. Set check-in times in advance.

Pro Tip

Download the official Lolla app and the offline version of the schedule before you arrive. Cell data speeds inside the festival can be painfully slow. Having the schedule accessible offline saves frustration.

After-Shows

Lolla after-shows are a beloved tradition. Artists from the festival play intimate late-night sets at venues across Chicago — clubs, theaters, and bars that hold a fraction of the festival crowd. After-shows are ticketed separately and sell out fast. If there's an artist you love, the after-show is often a better experience than their festival set: smaller room, longer setlist, more energy. Keep an eye on announcements from venues like Metro, Concord Music Hall, and Thalia Hall.

The Bottom Line

Lollapalooza is a marathon, not a sprint. The people who have the best time are the ones who pace themselves: they hydrate, they take breaks in the shade, they don't try to see 15 acts a day, and they leave room for the unexpected. Grant Park in late July is a special place — the skyline, the lake breeze, the energy of a few hundred thousand people all there for the same reason. Enjoy it.
